Two-NTC Constant-ΔT Flow Sensor — Model Explorer

Explore the DoubleNTC topology with a fully-digital (ESP32-software) control loop: an upstream NTC reads the stream temperature while a downstream NTC, warmed by a separate 40 W heater, is held a constant ΔT above it by a software PI driving a LEDC PWM. The power needed to hold ΔT is the flow signal (King's law, P ≈ a + b·√v), computed in firmware as duty × V_bus² / R — it never saturates and is intrinsically temperature compensated. See design/DoubleNTC.md and the single-probe companion ../model-explorer/.

Numerics

How the constant-ΔT loop works (fully digital, ESP32 software)

  1. Two jobs, separated. The NTCs only sense (low-current 3.3 V dividers, negligible self-heating); a separate 40 W ceramic heater on the 12 V bus does the heating. There are no op-amps, no bridge, no current-sense amplifier — the ESP32 runs the entire loop in software.
  2. Three ADC reads. ADC0 = upstream NTC divider, ADC1 = downstream NTC divider, ADC2 = a divided-down tap of the 12 V bus. The firmware converts the two divider voltages to T_up and T_dn with the Beta equation R = R25·exp(B(1/T − 1/T0)).
  3. True constant ΔT in software. The firmware computes T_target = T_up + ΔT and the error T_target − T_dn. Because ΔT is an explicit number (not a fixed resistor ratio), there is no Beta-ratio drift — the held ΔT is exact at every fluid temperature.
  4. Software PI → LEDC PWM. A software PI (output clamped 0..1, back-calculation anti-windup) turns the error into a duty and ledcWrite()s it to the gate driver → low-side MOSFET → heater. Flow rises → tip cools → T_dn drops → error → PI ramps the duty up → ΔT restored. Self-correcting at every flow.
  5. The flow signal is duty × V_bus² / R. The duty is known (the firmware commanded it), so no current sense is needed. Power ∝ V², and the marine bus swings ~11–15 V, so the firmware uses the measured V_bus from ADC2 — without that, a ±ΔV swing would give a ±2·ΔV/V power error.
  6. Hardware fail-safe. Software now owns 40 W, so a hardware watchdog forces the gate OFF if the firmware hangs, and a thermal cutout on the aluminium block is the final backstop. (The trade vs the analog loop: simpler + programmable + true constant ΔT + power-for-free, at the cost of ADC-noise-limited ΔT and dependence on MCU uptime.)

Flow calibration — power to hold ΔT vs flow rate

The core curve: electrical power the loop must deliver to hold ΔT as flow rises. Air and still-water levels are reference bands; the marker is the current flow. The dashed line is the King's-law fit P = a + b·√v — the power keeps climbing with flow (no saturation), which is the whole point versus the single probe.

Heater duty cycle vs flow

What fraction of the 40 W heater the PWM driver must use to hold ΔT. When the curve reaches 100 % the heater is saturated and can no longer hold ΔT at that flow — a feasibility limit (raise the heater power, lower ΔT, or use a smaller element).

Detection levels — power by regime

Air sits near the parasitic-only power floor; still water is higher; flowing water is higher still and rises with flow. Wide separation ⇒ robust air / still / flow discrimination from the power signal alone.

Why two sensors — ±1σ flow resolution vs the single probe

Both signals read through the same ADC. The single heated probe's head→fluid temperature difference shrinks with flow, so its flow resolution blows up at high flow. The CTD power signal only loses sensitivity as 1/√v, so its resolution stays bounded. Lower is better.

Flow-rate accuracy — ±1σ uncertainty vs flow (CTD)

ADC noise on the two NTC dividers sets a noise on the measured ΔT (σ_ΔT = √(σ_Tup²+σ_Tdn²)), which propagates to flow through the calibration; a smaller duty/Vbus-resolution term adds in quadrature. Degrades gracefully with flow. Improve it with a bigger ΔT, more averaging (the plant is slow), or a lower-noise ADC.

ΔT trade-off — power & resolution vs chosen ΔT

A bigger ΔT means a stronger signal (better resolution) but more power and, in water, a smaller margin to boiling. The two curves show the tension. (Right axis is relative.)

Temperature compensation — calibration vs fluid temperature

Power-vs-flow curves at several fluid temperatures (5–30 °C). Because ΔT is referenced to the upstream NTC and the physics is dominated by h·A·ΔT, the curves nearly coincide — the sensor is intrinsically temperature compensated (no heater-off ambient measurement needed, unlike the single probe). Residual spread is from fluid-property drift, not modelled here.

Turn-on transient — element temperature vs time

Cold start: the loop drives the element (up to the rail power) to the ΔT set-point. The small element mass settles fast; flowing water needs the most power and the bead settles faster than the tube.

Flow-step response — power signal vs time

Simulated with the actual software PI on the two-node thermal plant (heater block + element coupled through R_cond). On a flow change the loop re-settles ΔT and the measured power moves to the new value, dominated by the slow ~8.5 s thermal pole — this sensor responds over seconds.

V_drive compensation — power error vs bus swing

The flow signal is duty × V_bus² / R, so it is quadratic in the bus voltage. Without measuring V_bus, the marine bus swing (11–15 V) injects a ±2·ΔV/V power error; reading V_bus on ADC2 cuts it to the V_bus-ADC accuracy. Lower is better.
